I was born in a middle-class family in China, where expectations — from parents, teachers, peers, and society — often speak louder than one’s own inner voice. At 18, I left home for Scotland to study finance. When the pandemic hit, I turned to epidemiology and Population Health at UCL, trying to make sense of a world suddenly shaped by uncertainty. After gaining my bachelor and master degrees, I worked for two and a half years as a cancer data scientist in a specialist cancer hospital in London.

Hospitals change people. Watching both young and old lives pass made me understand something quietly but powerfully: I wanted to do work that brings people joy — and I wanted to create. In those long clinical corridors, I began to remember who I actually was.

I have loved games for as long as I can remember. On my very first day of primary school, my friends and I crowded around the old classroom computer to play games — and it happened to be the era when China’s web games were exploding. In sixth grade, I discovered my first AAA title, Tomb Raider. My mother is a film lover, so I grew up watching movies from every genre and era. In middle school, I wrote my first novella — a tragic reinterpretation of 20,000 Leagues Under the Sea. My joy has always been tied to worlds that are invented, imagined, and built.

So in late September 2025, I left the hospital. I opened VS Code again — not to build dashboards for doctors and nurses, not to model survival rates, and not for promotion or performance — but for myself. I dug out the scripts I wrote years ago and started creating the worlds I had long postponed.

Once I gave myself permission to return to games, my entire life shifted. I began teaching myself C# so I could work in Unity; I started learning sketching fundamentals to improve my ability to visualize characters and spaces; and I read everything I could to build a foundation — Game Design Theory, Designing Virtual Worlds, and Game Design Workshop. For the first time in years, I felt movement, curiosity, and a sense of direction that belonged wholly to me.

I want to study at CGL because of a moment in Cologne. During a business trip, after a conference, I wandered into the hall of Gamescom. Surrounded by creators from around the world, I saw one beautiful world after another unfolding on screens — worlds born from courage, imagination, and the desire to reach strangers’ hearts. For the first time, I felt clearly that I didn’t want to stand outside these worlds as a spectator. I wanted to be one of the people who build them.

I think there are real answers about what games are and how they work, waiting to be discovered, and I just need a chance to try.

Playable Prototypes

Three small pixel-art games built in Python. Each prototype focuses on a specific aspect of moment-to-moment gameplay: patterns and pressure, risk–reward, and 1v1 competitive rhythm against AI.

Alien Invasion
Top-down pixel shooter with waves and patterns
Solo Developer

Core idea: a wave-based top-down shooter focused on movement reading and projectile timing.

The player moves horizontally while firing upward. Alien fleets move as a formation, reverse direction when reaching screen edges and drop down a row. Enemies fire projectiles at timed intervals. The player clears waves to increase difficulty in terms of speed, bullet density and fleet spacing, and wins when a score threshold is reached.

Programming features:

  • Fleet movement system with edge-detection and vertical row shifts
  • Bullet pooling and collision handling for player and enemy projectiles
  • Scoring, level progression and difficulty scaling logic
  • Sprite groups, update loops and screen refresh in Pygame

Fish's Feast
Size-based growth and avoidance
Solo Developer

Core idea: a growth and avoidance game where distance to danger directly affects progression.

The player controls a fish that grows by consuming smaller fish while avoiding larger predators that end the run on collision. As the player grows, speed, hitbox size and movement weight change. Fish spawn from screen edges with randomized speed and direction. The game is won once the player reaches the target size.

Programming features:

  • Size-based collision rules using radius and bounding-box comparison
  • Dynamic scaling of movement speed and friction as the player grows
  • Randomized enemy spawning logic and off-screen clean-up
  • Growth curve tuning and hitbox recalculation per frame

Neon Puck Arena
Air-hockey vs AI in a neon arena
Solo Developer

Core idea: a fast-paced air-hockey game where the player controls the bottom paddle and plays against an AI.

The bottom paddle follows player input. The AI paddle tracks the puck using prediction with clamp-speed limits. The puck accelerates after each collision and bounces using angle-reflection physics. Scoring occurs when the puck enters a goal zone; the round then resets with updated velocity.

Programming features:

  • AI movement heuristics based on puck direction prediction
  • Elastic collision response for puck, paddles and arena walls
  • Vector-based speed, acceleration and reflection updates
  • Full game loop: input → AI update → physics → scoring → reset

Narrative Worlds

Concept documents for narrative-driven games exploring psychological themes, historical tragedy and systemic mechanics.

Not a Bug

Narrative Adventure · Psychological Transformation

Hook: When the world stops seeing you as human, how do you keep seeing the human in yourself?

Not a Bug is a narrative adventure set in a hyper-modern, almost cyberpunk version of New York, where the protagonist awakens in the body of a firefly-like creature. Struggling with alienation, disconnection and a profound loss of voice, he travels through alleys, rooftops, tunnels, abandoned offices and the hidden roots beneath parks.

Along the way, he meets other “bug-people”—former humans who quietly slipped out of society. Each encounter combines narrative choice with gameplay systems that mirror emotional states: foraging, pattern puzzles, corridor loops, rhythm and light.

A dynamic balance between Humanity and Insect Instinct determines whether he returns to human form or remains a bug.

Key design aspects:

  • Movement modes and energy management tied to transformation
  • Chapter-specific mechanics based on each NPC’s psychology
  • Humanity Index influenced by memory, trust and energy
  • UI distortion and environmental storytelling for emotional expression

Codename Jeanne

Historical Stealth-Action · Tragedy · Theology of Power

Hook: A hired assassin hunts a peasant girl destined for sainthood—only to confront the machinery of power.

Codename Jeanne is a historical stealth-action narrative set in 15th-century France. The player becomes an English assassin—code name The Crow—charged with eliminating Jeanne d’Arc, whose military success has turned her into both an asset and a threat.

Through ruins, battlefields and political courts, the assassin uncovers letters and secret archives showing how Jeanne’s rise—and her death—are shaped by nobles, clerics and generals driven by fear and ambition.

The story draws on the tragic logic of martyrdom and existential theology: in a world ruled by violence, even faith becomes an act of defiance.

Key design aspects:

  • Stealth–action hybrid system
  • Posture / SP model with vulnerability and execution
  • Weapon upgrade trees
  • Morale and faith influence on battlefield states
  • Evidence-driven narrative progression
