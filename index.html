I was born in a middle-class family in China, where expectations — from parents, teachers, peers, and society — often speak louder than one’s own inner voice. At 18, I left home for Scotland to study finance. When the pandemic hit, I turned to Epidemiology and Population Health at UCL, trying to make sense of a world suddenly shaped by uncertainty. After gaining my bachelor and master degrees, I worked for two and a half years as a cancer data scientist in a specialist cancer hospital in London.

Hospitals change people. In those long clinical corridors, with the echoes of the footsteps of the living and the departed, I began to remember who I actually was: my purpose was not to be found in the utility of data, but in the joy discovered through the act of creation.

My vocation took root on my very first day of primary school, huddled with friends around a classroom computer amidst the explosion of China’s web games. Raised on my mother’s eclectic film collection and the formative impact of Tomb Raider, I soon transitioned from player to author, penning a tragic reinterpretation of 20,000 Leagues Under the Sea. Whether through code or prose, my joy has always been anchored in worlds invented, imagined, and built.

In late September 2025, I left the hospital. I opened VS Code again — no longer to build dashboards for doctors and nurses, or to model survival rates, but for myself. I dug out the scripts I wrote years ago and started creating the worlds I had long postponed.

I immersed myself in C# and Unity, disciplined my hand with sketching fundamentals to visualise character and space, and established a theoretical bedrock through the study of the game design canon — from Bartle’s virtual worlds to Fullerton’s Game Design Workshop. I had finally found a direction that was, for the first time, wholly my own.

I want to study games because of a moment in Cologne. Following a business conference, I wandered into the halls of Gamescom. Surrounded by creators from around the world, I saw one universe after another unfolding on screens — worlds born from courage, imagination, and the desire to reach strangers’ hearts. In that moment, the boundary dissolved: I felt clearly that I didn’t want to stand outside as a spectator, but to be the architect within them.

I think there are real answers about what games are and how they work, waiting to be discovered, and I just need a chance to try.

AI Disclosure & Process

The conceptual illustrations for "Not a Bug" and "Codename Jeanne" were generated with the assistance of OpenAI’s DALL-E 3 & 4.

My Creative Process:

  • First, I conceptualized the required scenes based on my original narratives.
  • I created hand-drawn drafts to establish the desired composition.
  • Finally, I provided specific prompts to the model and manually performed digital touch-ups to fix any visual artifacts.

Note: All dialogue, world-building, and core concepts are 100% original, derived from my completed serialized novel.
